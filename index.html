Eusociality and Haplodiploidy in Hymenoptera

Ankush Sharma1,3, John E. Bowers1, Andrew H. Paterson1

1. Plant Genome Mapping Laboratory, University of Georgia, Athens, GA, 30602, USA

3. Institute of Bioinformatics, University of Georgia, Athens, GA, 30602, USA

Introduction

Eusociality and haplodiploidy have been strong drivers of genome stability and species diversification among insects. This study delves into the genomic intricacies of hymenopteran species, including ants, bees, and wasps, exploring how these social structures influence genetic conservation and biodiversity.

What We Know So Far

Prior research has established the significance of eusocial behavior in promoting genetic diversity and stability. Haplodiploidy, a unique sex determination mechanism, plays a crucial role in maintaining genomic integrity by eliminating deleterious alleles. These factors collectively contribute to the evolutionary success of Hymenoptera.

  • Eusociality enhances cooperation and division of labor within colonies.
  • Haplodiploidy facilitates effective purging of harmful recessive alleles.
  • Genomic stability is linked to reduced mutation rates and stronger purifying selection.

Methods

This study analyzed chromosome-scale genomes from various hymenopteran species using comparative genomics and Phylogenetic Generalized Least Squares (PGLS) models. These models account for shared evolutionary histories, enabling a robust analysis of syntenic conservation across social and nonsocial species.

Key Techniques:
  • Comparative Genomic Analysis
  • Phylogenetic Generalized Least Squares (PGLS) Models
  • Chromosome-Scale Genome Sequencing

Results

Eusocial Hymenoptera exhibit significantly higher levels of syntenic conservation compared to their nonsocial counterparts and other insect groups. The PGLS analysis confirms the strong association between social complexity and genomic stability, highlighting the role of haplodiploidy in maintaining conserved gene order.

Conclusion

This study elucidates the pivotal role of eusociality and haplodiploidy in maintaining high syntenic conservation within Hymenoptera. These findings advance our understanding of the genetic mechanisms that underpin speciation and long-term genome integrity in socially complex insect lineages.

Future research integrating population genomics, behavioral assays, and broader phylogenetic sampling will further unravel the interactions between haplodiploidy, social evolution, and chromosomal structure across diverse hexapod taxa.
